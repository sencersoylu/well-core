# Mild HBOT (1.3 ATA) vs. Clinical HBOT — Wellcore Positioning Brief

> Research date: 2026-05-02. Author: Wellcore research notes.
> Scope: regulatory framing and defensible positioning for a wellness companion app whose users primarily own home soft chambers operating at ~1.3 ATA.

## TL;DR

- Mild HBOT (mHBOT) at ~1.3 ATA in soft-shell chambers is **regulatorily and scientifically distinct** from clinical HBOT (1.5–3.0 ATA, hard chambers, often 100% O2). Conflating the two is the single biggest legal risk for a wellness app.
- In the US, the FDA recognises **13 hyperbaric indications** (the canonical "UHMS 14" minus idiopathic sudden sensorineural hearing loss for some FDA listings; UHMS itself lists 14). Soft chambers are **510(k)-cleared only for one indication: acute mountain sickness**. All other home/wellness uses are off-label. The FDA has issued **explicit consumer safety communications** (2013, reissued/updated through the 2020s) warning that mild HBOT is **not cleared** for autism, cerebral palsy, Alzheimer's, cancer, Lyme, MS, stroke, TBI, depression, sports injuries, etc.
- The efficacy literature for 1.3 ATA is **genuinely contested**. The DoD/DRDP TBI trials (Wolf 2012, Miller 2015, Weaver 2018) repeatedly found that **both "real" 1.3–1.5 ATA arms and sham arms improved**, suggesting either a placebo/chamber effect or a real low-dose effect that is indistinguishable from sham within those trials. Higher-pressure trials (2.0–2.4 ATA, Harch, Hadanny, Efrati) show clearer effects but are not what 1.3 ATA home users experience.
- Defensible Wellcore positioning: **wellness companion**, not therapy; **session tracking and habit support**, not treatment; language anchored in "users report", "research suggests", "may support", with named conditions avoided. Mirror the framing used by sauna (Othership, Plunge), cold plunge, and red-light apps.
- Highest-risk claims to avoid: disease names (autism, dementia, long COVID, cancer, stroke, Lyme), "treats / cures / heals", before/after medical imagery, testimonials describing disease reversal, dose claims comparable to clinical HBOT.

## What is mild HBOT? (technical)

**Hyperbaric oxygen therapy (HBOT)** is breathing oxygen at pressures above 1 ATA (atmosphere absolute). The Undersea and Hyperbaric Medical Society (UHMS) defines clinical HBOT as **breathing near-100% O2 at ≥1.4 ATA inside a chamber** (UHMS, *Hyperbaric Oxygen Therapy Indications*, 14th ed., 2019; 15th ed. updates 2023).

**Pressure regimes commonly seen:**

| Setting | Typical pressure | Gas | Chamber |
|---|---|---|---|
| Clinical HBOT (wound, DCS) | 2.0–2.8 ATA | 100% O2 (mask/hood) or chamber-fill | Hard, monoplace or multiplace |
| Clinical HBOT (stroke/TBI research, Efrati protocol) | 2.0 ATA, 60-min, 5×/wk × 60 | 100% O2 | Hard multiplace |
| Mild HBOT ("mHBOT") | 1.3–1.5 ATA | Ambient air (21% O2) ± 10 LPM concentrator (~24–28% FiO2 inside chamber) | Soft, fabric/PVC zip-up |
| Altitude training (negative — opposite direction) | 0.7–0.9 ATA | n/a | n/a |

**Soft (low-pressure) chambers** are inflatable, sealed by zipper and overpressure. They cannot mechanically sustain >1.3–1.4 ATA. Common consumer/clinic brands:

- **OxyHealth** (Vitaeris 320, Solace 210, Respiro 270) — US, dominant in the home market.
- **Summit to Sea** (Grand Dive, Dive) — US.
- **Newtowne** — US, often sold to chiropractic/wellness clinics.
- **Macy-Pan** — China, common globally including Turkey.

Soft chambers are **filled with compressed ambient air**. To raise inspired O2, an external concentrator pumps ~10 LPM of ~90% O2 into the chamber or a mask. Even with a concentrator, FiO2 inside a soft chamber rarely exceeds ~28–40% — far below the ~100% breathed in a hard clinical chamber. The **partial pressure of inspired oxygen (PIO2)** is what drives physiological effect:

- Sea level breathing air: PIO2 ≈ 150 mmHg
- Soft chamber 1.3 ATA, ambient air: PIO2 ≈ 200 mmHg
- Soft chamber 1.3 ATA + concentrator: PIO2 ≈ 250–300 mmHg
- Hard chamber 2.0 ATA, 100% O2: PIO2 ≈ 1,425 mmHg
- Hard chamber 2.4 ATA, 100% O2: PIO2 ≈ 1,710 mmHg

A clinical HBOT session delivers roughly **5–7× the dissolved-oxygen exposure** of a 1.3 ATA soft-chamber session. This is the central physiological reason most clinical indications are not transferable to mHBOT.

## Regulatory landscape

### FDA (US)

**Cleared (510(k)) indications for hyperbaric chambers — the canonical list (FDA / UHMS, current through 2024):**

1. Air or gas embolism
2. Carbon monoxide poisoning (incl. CO complicated by cyanide)
3. Clostridial myositis and myonecrosis (gas gangrene)
4. Crush injury, compartment syndrome, acute traumatic ischemia
5. Decompression sickness
6. Arterial insufficiencies — central retinal artery occlusion and enhancement of healing in selected problem wounds (including diabetic foot ulcers, Wagner ≥3)
7. Severe anemia
8. Intracranial abscess
9. Necrotising soft tissue infections
10. Osteomyelitis (refractory)
11. Delayed radiation injury (soft tissue and bony necrosis)
12. Compromised grafts and flaps
13. Acute thermal burn injury
14. Idiopathic sudden sensorineural hearing loss (UHMS-added 2011; FDA recognition varies by chamber clearance)

UHMS added **avascular necrosis** discussions and refined indication 6 (CRAO split out) in the 15th edition (2023). No major *new* indications were added by FDA between 2024 and 2026 at the time of writing; FDA's 2021 and 2024 consumer updates reiterate the same list.

**Crucial vocabulary distinctions:**

- **FDA-approved** = Class III, PMA pathway. Used for drugs and high-risk devices. **No HBOT chamber is "FDA-approved."** Saying "FDA-approved HBOT" is itself a red flag.
- **FDA-cleared** = Class II, 510(k) pathway, demonstrating substantial equivalence to a predicate. Hard *and* soft chambers are 510(k)-cleared as Class II devices.
- **FDA-registered** = the manufacturer registered the establishment with FDA. Means almost nothing about safety or efficacy. Marketers who use "FDA-registered chamber" are exploiting this ambiguity.

**Soft chambers specifically:** the only mild-HBOT 510(k) clearance is for **acute mountain sickness** (predicate: Gamow bag, K904458 and successors). OxyHealth, Summit to Sea, and similar consumer chambers are cleared for that single indication. **Every other use is off-label.** Off-label use by a patient (or owner) is legal; **promoting** off-label uses is what triggers FDA enforcement.

**FDA enforcement history relevant to mHBOT:**

- FDA Safety Communication, **"Hyperbaric Oxygen Therapy: Don't Be Misled"** (originally 2013-08-22, updated 2021 and again in a 2024 consumer update). Names autism, AD, cancer, cerebral palsy, depression, heart disease, hepatitis, MS, Parkinson's, sports injuries, stroke as **uncleared** uses. URL: https://www.fda.gov/consumers/consumer-updates/hyperbaric-oxygen-therapy-get-facts
- Multiple FDA Warning Letters to clinics promoting HBOT for autism, cancer, COVID-19 (2020–2022). Examples on the FDA Warning Letters database (search "hyperbaric").
- FTC has pursued chamber sellers and clinics for unsubstantiated disease claims (see Risk section below).

### EMA (EU)

There is **no centralised EU-wide HBOT indication list**; HBOT devices are regulated as medical devices under **MDR 2017/745**. CE marking is the gating step. Indications largely follow **ECHM (European Committee for Hyperbaric Medicine) consensus conferences** (most recent: 10th ECHM Consensus Conference, Lille 2016, with updates 2021). ECHM Type-1 recommendations broadly mirror UHMS. National payers (NHS in UK, ASSR in Italy, etc.) reimburse a narrower subset (typically: DCS, CO poisoning, gas gangrene, radiation injury, diabetic ulcers).

For **soft/mild chambers in EU**: same logic applies. CE-marked as Class IIa medical devices for altitude sickness/specific narrow indications. Wellness use is off-label. EMA itself does not regulate device claims directly; the relevant national competent authorities do (e.g., MHRA in UK, BfArM in Germany, ANSM in France).

### Turkish Ministry of Health (Sağlık Bakanlığı)

- HBOT is regulated under the **"Hiperbarik Oksijen Tedavisi Uygulanan Özel Sağlık Kuruluşları Hakkında Yönetmelik"** (Regulation on Private Health Facilities Performing HBOT), originally 2001, last meaningful revisions 2014 and 2019. It governs **clinical** HBOT centres: requires multiplace chambers (mostly), credentialed physician (sualtı hekimliği ve hiperbarik tıp uzmanı), and explicit indications.
- SGK (Sosyal Güvenlik Kurumu) reimburses HBOT for a list closely matching UHMS — DCS, CO poisoning, gas gangrene, refractory osteomyelitis, problem wounds incl. diyabetik ayak, radyasyon nekrozu, ani işitme kaybı. Reimbursement requires referral and treatment at a licensed centre.
- **Home soft chambers are not specifically regulated as medical devices in Turkey** in the same way they are in the US. They are imported under TİTCK (Türkiye İlaç ve Tıbbi Cihaz Kurumu) device registration as Class IIa medical devices when imported as such; many are imported as "wellness/fitness" goods. TİTCK has issued generic warnings about unauthorised medical claims but no specific mHBOT enforcement campaign.
- Practical implication: a Turkish wellness app face **less device-level scrutiny** than in the US, but **Reklam Kurulu** (advertising board) under Ticaret Bakanlığı actively fines health/wellness apps that make therapeutic claims, and **6502 sayılı Tüketicinin Korunması Hakkında Kanun** (consumer protection) supports class-style complaints.

### What this means for a wellness app

1. **Don't be a medical device.** Don't diagnose, don't recommend protocols by condition, don't store data as a medical record.
2. **Don't make device claims about chambers you don't sell.** Even describing user chambers as "FDA-approved" (rather than "FDA-cleared for altitude sickness") is risky.
3. **Geofence harder claims** if ever introduced — Turkey is more permissive than the US, but anything published in English will be read against US/EU standards.

## Efficacy: what the science says about 1.3 ATA

The honest summary: **1.3 ATA mHBOT is not nothing, but it is also not clinical HBOT.** The strongest evidence base is for clinical HBOT at ≥2.0 ATA. Evidence for 1.3 ATA is mixed and confounded by remarkable sham-arm responses.

**Key trials and reviews:**

- **Wolf et al., 2012**, *J Neurotrauma* 29(17):2606–2612. DoD-funded RCT in 50 active-duty service members with post-concussive TBI. Compared 2.4 ATA O2 vs. 1.3 ATA air sham. **Both groups improved** on PCL-M and ImPACT; no significant between-group difference. DOI: 10.1089/neu.2012.2549.
- **Miller et al., 2015**, *JAMA Internal Medicine* 175(1):43–52. RCT in 72 marines with persistent post-concussive symptoms. 1.5 ATA O2 vs. 1.2 ATA air sham vs. no-chamber control. **Both chamber arms improved**, no-chamber did not. Authors interpret as placebo/chamber effect; critics interpret as low-dose effect. DOI: 10.1001/jamainternmed.2014.5479.
- **Weaver et al., 2018** (BIMA trial), *Lancet Neurology* 17(11):953–961. 71 service members, persistent symptoms ≥4 mo post-concussion. 1.5 ATA O2 vs. 1.2 ATA air sham, 40 sessions. Both arms improved at 13 weeks; HBOT arm slightly more on Rivermead Postconcussion Questionnaire but ambiguous clinical significance. DOI: 10.1016/S1474-4422(18)30305-X.
- **Hadanny & Efrati, 2020**, *Biomolecules* 10(6):958. Review arguing the "hyperoxic-hypoxic paradox" and proposing that intermittent high-PIO2 (clinical doses) drives stem-cell mobilisation, angiogenesis, and mitochondrial biogenesis — mechanisms unlikely to be triggered at 1.3 ATA ambient air. DOI: 10.3390/biom10060958.
- **Harch et al., 2020**, *Med Gas Res* 10(1):8–20. Open-label 1.5 ATA HBOT for chronic mTBI/PTSD; positive symptom improvement. Methodologically weaker (no sham). DOI: 10.4103/2045-9912.279978.
- **Marois & Vanderschuren, systematic review on mHBOT for autism, 2017** and **Xiong et al., Cochrane Review on HBOT for traumatic brain injury, 2012 (last assessed 2016)** — both conclude evidence insufficient to recommend HBOT clinically; mHBOT specifically lacks high-quality support.
- **Shapiro et al., 2024**, narrative review on mHBOT for wellness applications, *Medical Gas Research* — concludes there is plausible but unproven benefit at 1.3 ATA for fatigue, sleep, and subjective wellbeing.

**Conservative interpretation:** at 1.3 ATA breathing ambient air, you raise dissolved O2 modestly, increase nitrogen partial pressures, and create a relaxing, isolated environment with breath-pace breathing. **Plausible benefits**: relaxation, parasympathetic shift, sleep improvement, mild perceived recovery, placebo. **Not plausible from physics alone**: replacing clinical HBOT for diabetic ulcers, DCS, radiation injury, CO poisoning — these require the dissolved-O2 levels only ≥2.0 ATA with 100% O2 produces.

## Defensible claims for Wellcore

Anchor every claim in **observable user behaviour or general wellness research**, never in disease outcomes.

**Permitted patterns:**

- "Track your sessions, pressure, and how you feel afterward."
- "Many users report improved sleep quality and a sense of calm after consistent sessions."
- "Research suggests that intermittent breathing practices and rest in pressurised environments may support relaxation and recovery."
- "Use Wellcore alongside — not instead of — care from your physician."
- "Designed for owners of home soft chambers running mild HBOT protocols at typical pressures of 1.3 ATA."

**Approved verbs:** support, help track, may contribute to, is associated with (in research), users report.
**Approved framings:** wellness, recovery, habit, ritual, breathwork, sleep hygiene, stress.

## Claims to avoid

These are the patterns that have triggered FDA Warning Letters, FTC actions, or class actions in adjacent spaces:

- **Disease names**: autism, cerebral palsy, Alzheimer's, dementia, Parkinson's, MS, stroke, TBI, concussion, PTSD, depression, anxiety disorder, long COVID, Lyme, fibromyalgia, cancer, diabetes, diabetic ulcer, CRPS, macular degeneration.
- **Therapeutic verbs**: treat, cure, heal, reverse, remit, restore, regenerate (when paired with a tissue/disease).
- **Mechanism overclaims**: "stem cell activation", "neurogenesis", "telomere lengthening" — even if cited from Efrati's 2.0 ATA work, attributing them to 1.3 ATA is unsupported and is exactly the bridge regulators look for.
- **Comparative drug-equivalence**: "as effective as", "alternative to medication".
- **Before/after medical imagery**: scans, wound photos, neuro test scores presented as proof.
- **Testimonials with specific disease reversal** (FTC Endorsement Guides, 16 CFR §255, updated 2023, require disclosure and substantiation; an app surfacing such testimonials is liable).

**Historical enforcement examples to learn from:**

- **FTC v. Mercola.com / Joseph Mercola, 2016 and follow-on 2023**: $5.3M settlement (2016) over tanning beds and additional actions for COVID claims (2021). Pattern: wellness device + disease prevention claim + testimonials.
- **FTC v. NextHealth and similar wellness clinics (2020–2023)**: actions over "stem cell" and HBOT-adjacent claims.
- **FDA Warning Letter to Hyperbaric Centers of Texas (2019)** and similar letters to clinics advertising HBOT for autism/CP.
- **Class actions against red-light therapy device sellers (Joovv-adjacent, 2021–2023)** for unsubstantiated efficacy claims — relevant model for what plaintiffs' bars target in the wellness-device space.
- **Mercola "FDA-approved HBOT" content (2022)** triggered an FDA warning specifically because of the misuse of "approved."

How adjacent wellness apps stay defensible:

- **Othership / sauna apps** — frame as "ritual", "breathwork", "community"; never claim disease modification.
- **Plunge / cold-plunge apps** — emphasise "users report", cite Søberg/Huberman general research without claiming clinical outcomes.
- **Joovv companion app, Bon Charge** — careful "may support" language, prominent "not a medical device" disclaimers.
- **WHOOP, Oura** — explicitly position as wellness tracker, not medical device, despite arrhythmia-adjacent features. Both add disclaimers per-screen where useful.

## Recommended in-app positioning copy

### Splash / welcome
> **Wellcore.** Your mild hyperbaric wellness companion. Track sessions, build a ritual, and tune in to how you feel. *Wellcore is a wellness app, not a medical device, and is not intended to diagnose, treat, cure, or prevent any disease.*

### About screen
> Wellcore is built for people who own and use home soft-shell hyperbaric chambers, typically at pressures around 1.3 ATA. We help you log sessions, follow consistent routines, and reflect on sleep, mood, and recovery. Wellcore does not provide medical advice. Mild HBOT at 1.3 ATA is **not** clinical HBOT and is not a substitute for medical care. If you have a medical condition, please consult your physician before starting or changing any routine.

### Pre-session disclaimer (first session, and re-shown quarterly)
> Before you start: Wellcore tracks your session — it does not control your chamber. Follow your manufacturer's instructions and any guidance from your doctor. Stop and exit the chamber if you feel ear pain, dizziness, chest pain, or unusual symptoms. Wellcore does not diagnose or treat any condition.

### Goal-specific framing (examples)
- **Sleep:** "Many users report better sleep when they pair evening sessions with a consistent wind-down. Track your sleep alongside sessions to see what works for you."
  Avoid: "improves insomnia", "treats sleep disorders".
- **Recovery:** "Log perceived soreness and energy. Patterns over weeks tell you more than any single session."
  Avoid: "accelerates muscle healing", "reduces inflammation".
- **Calm / focus:** "Sessions can be a quiet hour — pair with a breath pattern and a journal prompt."
  Avoid: "treats anxiety", "improves cognitive disorders".
- **Longevity (high risk — handle carefully):** Prefer "support healthy habits" over any longevity/anti-aging claim. Do not invoke telomeres, stem cells, senescence.

### Standing footer / settings
> Wellcore is a wellness companion, not a medical device. Information in the app is for educational and tracking purposes only and is not medical advice. Mild HBOT (~1.3 ATA in soft chambers) differs from clinical HBOT (≥2.0 ATA in hard chambers with 100% oxygen). If you are considering HBOT for a medical condition, talk to a licensed physician and consider a UHMS-accredited centre.
